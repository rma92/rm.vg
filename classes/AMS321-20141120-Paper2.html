  • The Second Red Scare and McCarthyism
    • Rosenberg Trial: FBI and Court attempted to indite Ethel Rosenberg in order to get Julius Rosenberg to admit to sharing weapon designs with the Soviets. David Greenglass was encouraged by the prosecution to turn over his brother in law (Julius), by telling a false testimony. The inditement of Ethel failed to cause Julius to come forward. Deputy D.A.: "She called our bluff."
    • McCarthy's politics produced a list of people who were to be found guilty by association, something which lacks circumstantial evidence. This type of law appeared in the 1300s in England, and was later abolished due to its ridiculous nature (video). It was also used by the Nazis (who were, of course, a bit ridiculous by American standards). Guilt by association has little to no stable, legal grounds for prosecution, but was used on several occasions. (Trial of Milo Radulovich, classified evidence, ridiculous grounds)
    • McCarthy's politics also caused the Attorney General (?) to be required to maintain a list of "communist" and "communist sympathizing" organizations and people. List had no stable legal background, but was used in court as a means of indicting people. (First Amendment is supposed to guarantee freedom of speech, even to go against the government. You are allowed to go against the government until it becomes an "imminent and pursuant threat" to violently overthrow the government, which saying you are a communist is not an example of)
    • Resolution: McCarthyism fell, more scrutiny due to press now.
  • Comstock law / Freedom of speech / contraceptives
    • Margaret Sanger 1917 trial: Women did not have the right to copulate with contraceptives. (lack of reason given)
    • Sanger was also denied her first amendment rights, as the comstock law was found to supersede the first amendment. Constitution is "supreme law of the land", and should not be superseded by mere acts. She was prosecuted for disseminating literature in the mail, even when not actually using the mail.
    • Gentlemen had a particular bias against Sanger, evidenced by her physicians (in the 1920s) being ostracized from the general community of physicians in New York.
    • Resolution: Contraception became available and more accepted int he 1920s and later, after a law allowing contraception to be prescribed by physicians was passed. Her actions may have been against the "spirit of the law", but would later be found to be acceptable in many circles.
  • Shorter Work day, Minimum wage, Healthy/Safe working conditions.
    • Triangle Factory Fire: owners locked doors to prevent stealing, fire occurs. One level of workers evacuates promptly, the level above that was not informed of the fire, many died. Owners were basically given a slap on the wrist. Max Blanck and Isaac Harris were indicted for first- and second-degree manslaughter. Case was won by the defense declaring that the defense failed to prove that the owners knew the doors were locked, and was able to destroy the credibility of a survivor by having her repeat a testimony many times, and suggesting she had memorized it. Blanck profited from insurance after a successful civil suit against him, and was arrested for locking the doors to the factory, but only fined $20.
    • Muller vs. Oregon -- Women are physically disavdantaged, and need to be protected. It is the state's duty to protect them. This was unsatisfactory to feminists, as it placed a heavy emphasis on discrimination of the sexes. Similar to Sanger case, there may have been a bias here, as the ruling set a legal precedent to use sex differences.
    • Resolution: After Triangle factory, workplace laws became more favorable. Muller was tested in Adkins v Children's Hospital. More recently, the courts have been more favorable towards women, especially after the nineteenth amendment passed.
